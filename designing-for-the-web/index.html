Obvious solution

The best of the two worlds

developers and designers are masters in their field.

Developers know: limits of technology, cross-compatibility issues, browser capabilities, performance, device limits, semantics and supported features
Designers know: why using a grid rather than another, white space, vertical rhythm, typography, color theory, and so on.

Let's talk about the elephant in the room

Designers must sit with developers

Promote knowledge sharing and upskilling

Leverage on each other's knwoledge

This is not anymore a timid suggestion...
No more designs slipped under the devs' door.
UI development (or frontend design© Brad Frost) takes time and skills.

1-10-100 rule

Although 100 is more like in the order of 1.000.000

How many of you know this rule?
1: fixing at design time.
10: fixing in development.
100: fixing after delivery.

Iterate and validate

Always try to find room for improvement
Learn from mistakes
& remove obstacles as quickly as possible

Involve your users

they hold the key for fine-tuning your user experience

Designers and developers are BIASED
you HAVE to gather information on the use of your product.

Designing in the browser & Rapid Prototyping

choose the right level of fidelity

Design Sprints

Very quick (5 days) iteration process for discovering new features or brainstorming new ideas.

www.gv.com/sprint/

If you're worried iterations could be too costly

Use Style Guides & Pattern Libraries

This is probably one of the most discussed topics recently, and it's worth investigating as they will save you an incredible amount of time in the long run if used appropriately.
I've written about it and you can find more info on my blog.

Work bottom-up

Work top-down

Bottom-up have devs sit with designers, understand the tools of the trade, elevate devs to focus on frontend design.
Top-down build a use-case, with users in mind, accessibility, usability and device agnosticism.
